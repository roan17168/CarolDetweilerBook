Unlocking the Iron Door

The Scholarship Program That Changed 50+ Lives

The Story Behind the Legacy

In 1999, as refugees fled the devastation of the Kosova war, Carol Detweiler asked a simple question: What happens to the young people whose educations were stolen from them?

She didn't wait for someone else to answer. Within weeks, she had built a scholarship program from the ground up — partnering with American colleges and universities to offer full college scholarships to more than 50 Kosovar refugees. Students who had arrived in the United States with one bag of clothes walked into classrooms, dormitories, and futures they had been told were no longer possible.

Today, those students are teachers, doctors, actors, engineers, entrepreneurs, and parents raising their own children in freedom.

"I didn't do the work for them. I simply unlocked a heavy iron door they had to push through themselves." — Carol Detweiler

Recognition

In 2000, the New York State Senate honored Carol Detweiler with a "Women of Distinction" award for her extraordinary work on behalf of Kosovar refugees. But the true recognition, she says, is in the lives of the students themselves.
