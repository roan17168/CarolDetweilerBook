The Official Book Club Kit

Discussion Questions, Historical Context, and Reflection Prompts

A Murder of Crows Fly with a Red Ribbon is a novel meant to be discussed. Whether you're gathering with a book club, teaching in a classroom, or reflecting on your own, these questions are designed to open conversation about war, memory, displacement, and hope.

  1. The novel opens with Ariana recalling a peaceful Easter morning before moving into the escalating violence of the war. Why do you think the author chose to structure the story this way, rather than telling it in strict chronological order?
  2. The title draws on the historical meaning of "Kosovo" ("field of blackbirds") and the old term "a murder of crows." How does this imagery shape the tone of the novel? What do the crows come to represent by the end?
  3. The book includes historical figures — Milosevic, Rugova, Clinton, Madeleine Albright — alongside its fictional characters. How did weaving real history into a fictional narrative affect your reading experience?
  4. Detweiler explains in her preface that she combined and altered real refugees' experiences to protect their identities. Does knowing the story is "based on truth but not literally true" change how you engage with it?
  1. Ariana travels on false papers, essentially becoming someone else in order to survive. What does this forced loss of identity cost her, even before she reaches safety?
  2. Several characters are scattered across different countries — Kosova, Italy, Montenegro, England, New York. How does the novel portray the particular loneliness of not knowing where your family members are?
  3. For the characters who reach the United States, arriving safely doesn't mean the struggle is over. What did you notice about how the book portrays the emotional and cultural adjustment of resettlement, separate from the trauma of the war itself?
  4. The red ribbon and the small red knife are physical objects Ariana carries throughout her journey. What do you think they mean to her, and did that meaning shift for you as the story progressed?
  1. The Gashi brothers are separated by circumstance — some already abroad, some still in Kosova as danger closes in. How does the novel explore the guilt or helplessness of the family members who are safe while others are not?
  2. Faith and doubt both appear in Ariana's reflections, particularly around the cross her grandmother gave her. How does the novel handle the tension between hope and disillusionment during periods of extreme hardship?
  1. The novel is told through multiple perspectives and jumps between timelines and locations. Did you find this approach effective for conveying the scale and confusion of the war, or did you wish for a more linear telling?
  2. The book closes with an appendix including a historical timeline, President Clinton's speech, and information about the real scholarship program that helped resettle Kosovar students. Why might the author have chosen to include these alongside the fiction?
  1. What responsibility do you think fiction has in preserving histories that might otherwise be forgotten or under-covered?
  2. The author founded a real program helping Kosovar refugees attend U.S. colleges. How did knowing this personal connection to the events change how you read the novel?
  3. What does this novel add to your understanding of the Kosova war that a history book or news account might not capture?
